About this work

Neural Control: Adjoint Learning Through Equilibrium Constraints proposes a learning-based controller for soft, deformable structures whose dynamics are governed by static equilibrium constraints. Instead of unrolling expensive forward simulations through every optimization step, we cast control as an adjoint-driven learning problem and integrate it with receding-horizon control (RHC).

Problem setting

Many soft-structure manipulation tasks reduce to optimizing a control input \(z\) so that the equilibrium state \(\varepsilon^\star\), defined implicitly by \(G(\varepsilon^\star, z; \theta)=0\), tracks a target trajectory. Classical derivative-free methods (iCEM, SPSA) repeatedly evaluate the forward solver and scale poorly with horizon length; full backpropagation through the implicit solver is memory-prohibitive.

Method

  • Proxy-adjoint learning. We differentiate through equilibrium constraints via implicit differentiation and proxy sensitivity dynamics, computing trajectory-dependent gradients without unrolling equilibrium solver iterations.
  • Receding-horizon continuation. We optimize neural continuation policies over short horizons and repeatedly re-anchor to realized equilibria, improving robustness in long-horizon and multi-stable control.
  • Equilibrium-consistent control. The framework composes with physics simulators and learned equilibrium models while preserving branch-consistent equilibrium rollouts for equilibrium-governed systems.

Tasks

  1. Node targeting — drive a selected node of an elastic strip to a prescribed position.
  2. Trajectory tracing — have the middle node follow a prescribed curve over time.
  3. Shape control — deform the entire strip to match a target configuration.

Results

Across all three tasks, Adjoint + RHC delivers orders-of-magnitude better tracking loss than iCEM at a fraction of the wall-clock cost.

Validation on learned DEQ-style model

We additionally validate the approach on a learned equilibrium model \(E_\theta(\varepsilon)\) trained from force–strain measurements of a real slinky. Neural Control optimizes a force trajectory so the resulting equilibrium strain matches \(\varepsilon^*(\lambda)=0.05\sin(2\pi\lambda)+0.05\), achieving segment losses on the order of \(10^{-7}\)–\(10^{-8}\).
